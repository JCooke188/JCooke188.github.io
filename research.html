Research Interests

My primary area of research interests are within computational fluid dynamics. Specifically, I am would like to employ high performance computing (HPC) and numerical solvers to study aerodynamic and hydrodynamic flows. At present, my focus has been on low Reynolds number flows, but I would like to eventually work in turbulent rough-wall flows. Other areas of interest include employing data driven modeling techniques in the study and understanding of the physics behind non-equilibrium turbulent flows.

Current Projects

Low Reynolds Number Aerodynamics

I am currently conducting research on low Reynolds number flows for micro aerial vehicle flight (MAV). My involvement with the design team is to aid in the design and development of propellers for MAVs, using a finite volume flow solver, charles from Cascade Technologies, to analyze aerodynamic forces. Propeller design iterations with various geometric specifications are investigated to determine which characteristics are most favorable for MAV deployment. The present work is looking towards the flight capabilities of insects and other animals that live in the low Reynolds number regime. Much work has been done to review the underlying flow mechanics through an extensive literature review, in order to provide inspiration for the propeller geometry. Findings for this work are currently being prepared in an academic paper, which will be submitted to an academic journal soon.

Past Projects

Correlation Between Reynols Number and Rayleigh Number for Vertical Ellipse

Worked in a team investigating the possibility of a correlation between Reynolds Number, Re, and Rayleigh Number, Ra, for a vertical ellipse. It is known that an existing correlation exists for a cylinder, so investigation into similar geometries was conducted. I ran high fidelity simulations, accessing remotely high performance computers through a Linux based software. Data was extracted from the simulations and then used to visualize these high fidelity simulations, and create a relationship between Re and Ra. Findings were presented to the rest of the team.

The video on the left is a vertical ellipse that in natural convection, with a Rayleigh number of 100. Plumes can be seen once the ellipse has had heat applied to it, and then within a few moments the plumes reduce in size and the system reaches a steady state. The geometry and mesh were made using Nektar++, and the visual was created using EnSign.
